Used ThinkPad Buying Guide

Updated 2017-12-18. Written by sdx1 with inspiration from ktgee.net's guide. Originally published 2017-12-18.

Lenovo's ThinkPad business laptops, inherited from IBM, have a great reputation among businesses and enthusiasts alike. Though expensive new, they're cheap used, as many businesses dump them after 3-4 years. This guide is an overview of available models and price ranges (depending on specs & condition).

Jump to: Standard, Light, Tablet, Workstation, Cheap, Alternatives, Libreboot, Vintage, Additional Info

Why ThinkPads?

  • They're immensely durable & reliable. The magnesium rollcage & drainage holes shake off drops & spills.
  • Keyboards have comfortable ergonomics and quiet yet very tactile feedback.
  • Unashamed utilitarian designs: black boxy casing, indicator LEDs, desktop keyboard layout (pre-2012).
  • Drop-in docks allow easy connections to external devices at your desk.
  • Easy to repair/upgrade thanks to readily available documentation, community help, & parts.
  • The TrackPoint (red thing on the keyboard) is great for those who type a lot or hate touchpads.
  • Excellent support for *nix operating systems.

Why buy used?

  • Prices drop rapidly. A 3 year old ThinkPad can often be found at a quarter its original price.
  • Laptops don't improve quickly in performance. A 2016 i5 G7 is only 28% faster than a 2011 i5 G2.
  • They're already tested, which means you won't have to deal with Lenovo's mediocre quality control, shipping, and support. This means used ThinkPads are, uninituitively, more reliable than new ones.

Things to Know

  • eBay & Craigslist are your best bet: avoid pricey "refurbished" ThinkPads on Amazon & Newegg
  • Don't buy a ThinkPad with a BIOS password--always make sure it can enter setup. It's very hard to fix.
  • Make sure Computrace is off. Otherwise, your device can be tracked and remotely disabled.
  • Examine the screen - resolution, colors, and check for flaws at an angle while displaying gray.
  • Upon receipt or when buying in person: check battery wear, physical scratches/scuffs/cracks/flex/gaps/rattles, SSD/HDD life (CrystalDiskInfo), port functionality, speakers, CPU temperatures under heavy load (Prime95 + OpenHardwareMonitor).
  • Read reviews (click the models numbers below) before buying your ThinkPad. Each has its own quirks.
  • It's best to reinstall your OS to avoid bloat.
  • Windows 10 (download) still accepts Windows 7 keys from the bottom of your PC's casing.
  • SSDs make a huge difference in everyday use performance. Get a nice one for yourself.
  • It's often cheaper to buy extra RAM and an SSD seperately to upgrade a ThinkPad
  • TPMiddle is a lifesaver for TrackPoint users that want to scroll AND middle-click

T-series Standard Laptops

These general-purpose, 14-15" laptops have a great balance of performance, weight, & battery life.

  • T420   (14" Core i G2) - $80-$140 - common & widely acclaimed, classic keyboard, 900p display available
  • T420s (14" Core i G2) - $80-$140 - a thinner T420 with worse battery and USB3
  • T520   (15" Core i G2) - $80-$170 - a bigger T420
  • T430   (14" Core i G3) - $100-$200 - an upgraded T420 with a new-style keyboard and USB3
  • T430s (14" Core i G3) - $100-$200 - a thinner T430 with worse battery life
  • T530   (15" Core i G3) - $150-$250 - a bigger T430
  • T430u (14" Core i G3) - $150-$230 - a slight upgrade to the T430s
  • T431s (14" Core i G3) - $200-$300 - an oddity - a T430 in T440 casing
  • T440   (14" Core i G4) - $180-$300 - a rethought design and a buttonless touchpad that can be upgraded
  • T440s (14" Core i G4) - $250-$330 - a more premium, lightweight T440 with an IPS display
  • T440p (14" Core i G4) - $180-$300 or $500-$600 for quad-core version - last ThinkPad with socketed CPU
  • T540p (15" Core i G4) - $250-$440 - a big T440p (quad cores available)
  • T450   (14" Core i G5) - $250-$500 - just an evolution of the T440 with a better trackpad
  • T450s (14" Core i G5) - $300-$600 - a more premium, lightweight T450
  • T460   (14" Core i G6) - $450-$600 - another slight upgrade over the T450
  • T460s (14" Core i G6) - $550-$650 - a more premium, lightweight T460
  • T470   (14" Core i G6/7) - $625-$900 - a slight redesign resembling the T460s

s models (like the T420s) are more lightweight with worse battery life than their standard counterparts. Some have some premium additions (such as the T450s' IPS display, or the T420s' USB3 ports).

i models (like the T420i) have less powerful processors than their unlettered counterparts (usually i3s)

p models (like the T440p) are heavier with dedicated graphics, better battery life, and/or a quad-core CPUs

You can transplant a quad-core processor into a T420--don't. G2 mobile quads aren't much of an upgrade, and it's not worth the cost, effort, and overheating. Want a tiny quad-core laptop? T430, T440p, 2570p, or E6430.

X-series Smaller Lightweight Laptops

These smaller laptops make excellent portable machines. The X200 series has 12" displays with a thicker utilitarian body to fit ports on, while the X1 Carbon series are thin MacBook Air styled devices.

  • X220 (12" Core i G2) - $80-$135 - cheap, light, often considered the "last & best classic ThinkPad"
  • X1      (13" Core i G1) - $200-$300 - X1 Carbon's precursor - mediocre performance/battery, flimsy, light
  • X230 (12" Core i G3) - $80-$170 - an upgraded X220 with a new-style keyboard & USB3
  • X1C1 (13" Core i G3) - $200-$300 - X1 Carbon G1 - thin, light, fast, durable, mediocre sealed battery
  • X240 (12" Core i G4) - $160-$300 - a redesigned laptop with a buttonless touchpad you should replace
  • X1C2 (14" Core i G4) - $300-$450 - better X1C hindered by buttonless trackpad (replace) & F-keys
  • X250 (12" Core i G5) - $300-$500 - a directly upgraded X240 with the fixed trackpad
  • X1C3 (14" Core i G5) - $450-$900 - thin, light, fast, decent battery - perfection
  • X260 (12" Core i G6) - $550-$950 - a directly upgraded X240 with the fixed trackpad
  • 13      (13" Core i G7) - $500-$700 - somewhere between an X270 & X1C; marketed towards education

X-Series Tablet PCs (and other tablets)

Lenovo has tried a variety of methods to make tablet and tablet-convertible ThinkPads. Note that most everything past the X230 is going to lack a lot of attributes that make ThinkPads great, like easy upgradability or the magnesium rollcage.

  • X220t      (12" Core i G2) - $150-$300 - a heavier, tablet-convertible X220
  • X230t      (12" Core i G3) - $130-$350 - a heavier, tablet-convertible X230
  • Twist       (12" Core i G3) - $150-$300 - a ThinkPad keyboard on a consumer-grade laptop. Ugh.
  • Helix        (11" Core i G3) - $180-$300 - a Windows tablet with a ThinkPad-styled keyboard dock
  • S1 Yoga   (12" Core i G4) - $250-$350 - a good (though glassy) convertible with buttonless trackpad
  • Helix 2     (11" Core m G4) - $280-$500 - a fanless upgraded Helix
  • Yoga 11e (11" Celeron BT) - $100-$200 - a rugged, anemic subnotebook convertible for schools
  • Yoga 12   (12" Core i G5/6) - $250-$400 - the successor to the S1 Yoga
  • Yoga 14   (14" Core i G5/6) - $300-$550 - a big Yoga 12 with decent dedicated graphics
  • X1 Tablet (14" Core i G6) - $450-$750 - Lenovo's answer to the Microsoft Surface

W-Series Workstations

These 15"-17" desktop replacements will all have quad-core processors and most have some kind of dedicated graphics. These are designed to appeal to users requiring lots of computing power, like software developers and CAD users.

  • W520 (15" Core i G2) - $200-$550 - a great, if heavy, classic keyboard ThinkPad
  • W530 (15" Core i G3) - $220-$550 - an upgraded W520 with chiclet keys and a GPU similar to a GT 630M
  • W540 (15" Core i G4) - $400-$800 - redesign, buttonless trackpad, GPUs like GT 740M and 750M
  • W541 (15" Core i G4) - $600-$900 - just a W540 with a better trackpad (replace it yourself on the W540)

  • R400, R500, T400, T500, R61, T61 (non-P) (14"-15" Core 2 Duo) - $40-$120
  • T400 (14" Core 2 Duo) - $
  • T410 (14" Core i G2) - $50-$120

You'll find other ThinkPad laptops not listed here from the E, L, R, and SL series. While some of these are similar to T- and X-series models, others don't meet the quality standards most have associated with ThinkPads. If you want to buy one of these, make sure to do your research first.

Alternative Laptops

Hey, wait! These aren't ThinkPads!

Dell's Latitude and Precision lines, as well as HP's EliteBook line, both make great business laptop alternatives to ThinkPads. The keyboards and TrackPoints are hardly as good, and sometimes the build quality can be hit or miss. But as much as hardcore fans will yell blasphemy, they're not much less consistent than ThinkPads. You just have to know what to buy.

Libreboot ThinkPads

Libreboot is a free/open-source BIOS/UEFI replacement. Privacy-focused people and principled free software nerds appreciate its extensibility, unrestrictive licensing, and lack of backdoors. Due to some technical limitations imposed by Intel, only Core 2 Duo and older laptops can run Libreboot today--thus the obvious choice of laptops by most suppliers are old ThinkPads.

It might be worth noting that Coreboot (with proprietary blobs) has been successfully installed on the R400, S230U, T400, T420(s), T430(s), T500, T520, T530, T60(p), X1C G1, X200, X201, X220, X2300, X60(s), and X131e.

Vintage Thinkpads

Classic IBM ThinkPads, particularly from the 90s, are no doubt collectible. Particularly well known is the 701c, an early ThinkPad with a "butterfly" keyboard expanding as the laptop opens. Vintage ThinkPads tend to show up in thrift stores from time to time, but more commonly on eBay and in electronics recycling bins. One could ask around at some local computer shops and offer a small reward for any ThinkPad they might have otherwise sent with their recycler.

Bill Morrow's thinkpads.com Forum, VOGONS, ThinkWiki, and /r/ThinkPad are all excellent resources for learning about vintage ThinkPads.

Wireless Whitelists

Without BIOS mods, your ThinkPad will not boot if an internal wireless adapter other than adapters distributed by Lenovo is installed. Search "[model] wireless whitelist" for more information.

BIOS Mods

The T420 has a great BIOS mod available that unlocks options, enables 16GB RAM support, and removes the WLAN whitelist. Other ThinkPads are covered on ThinkWiki.

Hard Drive Upgrades

Not all ThinkPads use the same drive types, so check ThinkWiki for your model before you order a replacement hard drive or SSD. Laptops in the X220, X230, T420s, and T430 series are known to require 7mm slim 2.5" HDDs (the standard size for 2.5" SSDs), and the T400s, T410s, X300, and X301 take rare 1.8" drives, so you'll probably want an mSATA adapter and an appropriate mSATA SSD.

WWAN/mPCIe/M.2 Slots

If you don't need WWAN, many Thinkpads support SSDs in these slots. You'll need to consider the type of slot you have, whether your device accepts SSDs, whether it uses PCIe or SATA to communicate with the drive, and whether it can boot from an SSD in these slots. /r/ThinkPad can probably help you there, but do some research before you make a post.

Gaming on a ThinkPad

ThinkPads are not intended as gaming laptops. The best you can hope for is either an eGPU solution or a workstation model. Particularly, the P51 is known to handle gaming quite well, although you'll pay a hefty premium for it being a new workstation laptop, even with the corporate discount.

Regarding eGPUs, I had good luck running Overwatch, Skyrim, and Rocket League on my T420 with a GTX 950. It was connected through a PE4C v3.0 in the ExpressCard slot powered by a Dell DA-2 power supply, although it crashed on occasion. A search for "[thinkpad model number] eGPU" is typically fruitful if you want to find out if and how a ThinkPad would handle an eGPU.

Newer ThinkPads can handle some 3D gaming. Skyrim was smooth on my X1 Carbon G3 with Intel HD 5500 graphics.

Most ThinkPads will handle most 2D games, including Terraria, Osmos, Bastion, and Binding of Isaac. Check Humble Bundle and GOG for some great cheap 2D games.
